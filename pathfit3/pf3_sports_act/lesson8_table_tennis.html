Introduction to Table Tennis

Lesson Overview

Table tennis, also known as Ping-Pong, is a fast-paced sport played by either two (singles) or four (doubles) players. It is a sport of finesse, speed, and strategy, demanding high levels of coordination and quick reaction time.

This lesson introduces the rich history of table tennis, the official rules and equipment as governed by the ITTF, and the fundamental mechanics of gameplay, including serving, scoring, and stroke types.

History and Governance

Table tennis originated as an after-dinner pastime for the elite in England towards the end of the 19th century, evolving from makeshift equipment like cigar boxes and champagne corks.

Key Historical Points

  • Early Names: It was known as Gossima, Whif-Whaf (or wiff-waff), Flim Flam, and Ping-Pong, the latter due to the sound of the ball hitting the table and racket.
  • Olympic Status: Table tennis became an Olympic sport in 1988 in Seoul, South Korea, initially featuring singles and doubles competitions. The doubles event was replaced by the team event in 2008.
  • Governance: The sport is globally governed by the International Table Tennis Federation (ITTF), founded in 1926.

Major Rule Changes (Post-2000)

The ITTF instituted changes to slow down the game and increase rally length for better spectator viewing:

  • Ball Size: The older 38 mm ball was replaced by a 40 mm ball in October 2000, which increased air resistance and slowed the game down.
  • Scoring: The game was changed from a 21-point system to an 11-point scoring system (effective September 2001), aiming for faster-paced, more exciting matches.
  • Service Rule: Rules were changed in 2002 to prevent players from hiding the ball during service, requiring the ball to be tossed a minimum of 16 cm (6.3 in) high.

Equipment Specifications

Official equipment adheres to strict ITTF standards to ensure fair and consistent play.

Equipment Specifications Key Details
Table (Playing Surface) 2.74 m long, 1.525 m wide, 76 cm high Must yield a uniform bounce of about 23 cm when dropped from 30 cm. Must be uniformly dark and matt with 2 cm white side lines.
Net Assembly 15.25 cm high The net assembly includes the net, suspension, and supporting posts, which extend 15.25 cm outside the side line.
Ball 40 mm diameter, 2.7 g weight Made of celluloid or similar plastics, and must be white or orange and matt.
Racket Any size, shape, or weight The blade must be flat and rigid. At least 85% of the blade by thickness must be of natural wood. Common grips are Penhold and Shakehand.

Gameplay and Strokes

Matches start with the first serve decided by lot (e.g., coin toss). The ultimate goal is to win a point by forcing the opponent to commit a Fault or fail to make a legal return.

Types of Strokes

Different strokes are used to impart various speeds and spins on the ball:

  • BLOCK: Played defensively to control an attacking shot.
  • LOOP: Generates high topspin, causing the ball to dip quickly on the opponent's side.
  • CHOP: A stroke that produces heavy underspin on the ball.
  • SMASH: An offensive "trump card," a powerful, fast hit with minimal spin.

Service and Scoring Summary

  • Service: The ball must be tossed vertically at least 16 cm (6.3 in) high. It must touch the server's court once, then the receiver's court once, without hitting the net.
  • Service Alternation: Service alternates every two points until the end of the game, or until deuce (10-10), when service alternates every one point.
  • Ends: Players switch sides of the table after each game. In the final possible game, players switch ends when the first player scores five points.

Key Terminologies

  • RALLY: The period during which the ball is in play.
  • POINT: A rally which the result is scored (e.g., one player fails to make a legal return).
  • LET: A rally of which the result is not scored (e.g., the ball touches the net during a legal serve, or the receiver was not ready).
  • DEAD BALL: A ball that is not in play.
  • FAULT: Any error committed by a player (e.g., Double Bounce, Moved Table, Wrong Side in doubles service).
  • RECEIVER: The player due to strike the ball second in a rally.
