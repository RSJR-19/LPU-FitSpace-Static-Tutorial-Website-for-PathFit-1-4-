Introduction to Arnis (Filipino Martial Arts)

Lesson Overview

Arnis, also known as Eskrima or Kali, is the national martial art and sport of the Philippines. It is a complete martial arts system emphasizing weapon-based fighting using sticks, knives, and various improvised weapons, as well as empty-hand techniques.

This lesson introduces the rich history of Arnis, its core principles, essential equipment, and the fundamental striking and blocking techniques required for practice and competition.

History and Significance

Arnis originated from the native fighting methods used for self-defense and tribal warfare across the Philippine archipelago. It developed over centuries, adapting to different environments and foreign influences.

Key Historical Points

  • Names: The term Arnis comes from "Arnes de mano" (armor of the hands). Eskrima is derived from the Spanish word for fencing ("esgrima"). Kali is an indigenous term, often used to refer to the blade-based arts.
  • Survival Art: Arnis was used by Filipino heroes, notably Lapu-Lapu, who is credited with defeating Ferdinand Magellan in 1521.
  • National Sport: Arnis was declared the national martial art and sport of the Philippines in 2009 by virtue of Republic Act No. 9850, ensuring its preservation and promotion.

Equipment and Foundational Stances

Essential Equipment

  • Yantok (Stick): The primary training weapon, typically made from rattan, approximately 28 inches long and about $1/2$ to 1 inch in diameter.
  • Dulo-Dulo (Palm Stick): A short stick used for close-quarters striking and joint manipulation.
  • Bolo/Daga (Blade/Knife): Used in advanced training, but often represented by wooden or dull training blades.
  • Safety Gear: Headgear, padded vests, and arm/shin guards are required for competitive sparring.

Basic Stances

Stances are the foundation for balance, power, and agility in Arnis:

  • Ready Stance: Used when marching or standing at attention, holding the stick vertically at the side.
  • Attention Stance: Used for courtesy or when receiving instructions.
  • Forward Stance: Used for forward and backward movements; allows for quick lunges and retreats.
  • Straddle Stance (Horse Stance): Used for powerful strikes and blocking; provides maximum stability.
  • Back Stance: Used for quick side-to-side movements and retreats, shifting weight mostly to the rear leg.

The Twelve Basic Striking Areas

Arnis training is often structured around the 12 fundamental striking areas of the human body. Learning these targets teaches practitioners where to strike and, conversely, where to block and defend.

  • Left Temple: Diagonal strike to the left side of the head.
  • Right Temple: Diagonal strike to the right side of the head.
  • Left Shoulder/Arm: Horizontal strike across the left shoulder or arm.
  • Right Shoulder/Arm: Horizontal strike across the right shoulder or arm.
  • Stomach/Abdomen: Thrusting strike to the solar plexus.
  • Left Chest: Thrusting strike to the left chest area.
  • Right Chest: Thrusting strike to the right chest area.
  • Right Leg: Downward diagonal strike to the knee or thigh.
  • Left Leg: Downward diagonal strike to the knee or thigh.
  • Left Eye: Thrusting strike to the left eye.
  • Right Eye: Thrusting strike to the right eye.
  • Crown/Top of Head: Vertical strike directly on the head.

Fundamental Techniques: Blocking and Hand Grip

Defensive movements are just as critical as offensive strikes. Effective blocking requires a firm grip and proper footwork.

Blocking Techniques

  • Upward Block: Used to counter strikes aimed at the crown or vertically downwards.
  • Downward Block: Used to counter strikes aimed at the lower extremities (legs).
  • Inside Block: Used to defend against horizontal strikes coming from the outside towards the center.
  • Outside Block: Used to defend against strikes coming from the center outwards.

Did you know? The grip on the stick (yantok) must be firm enough to maintain control but loose enough to allow for wrist rotation for power and speed. Grip is typically held about 3-4 inches from the butt of the stick.

Key Takeaways

  1. Arnis (Eskrima/Kali) is the indigenous martial art of the Philippines, emphasizing weapon and empty-hand defense.
  2. The primary weapon is the Yantok (rattan stick), about 28 inches long.
  3. Mastering the basic stances (e.g., Forward, Straddle) ensures balance and power.
  4. All offensive and defensive training is built around the 12 fundamental striking areas.
  5. Arnis is a complete system focused on practical, real-world application, making it highly valuable for self-defense.
